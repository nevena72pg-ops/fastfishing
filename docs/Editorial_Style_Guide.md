# Editorial Style Guide

## Purpose

This guide protects the voice of FishWithLocals as the platform grows. It applies to captain profiles, destination pages, stories, practical information, calls to action, image captions, emails and interface text.

Every page should sound human, specific, calm and grounded in real experience. A reader should feel that someone paid attention before writing. Clear information matters, but accuracy alone is not enough: the words must also represent captains, guests, places and the sea with care.

Use this guide while drafting and again before publishing. When it conflicts with a fashionable phrase, search tactic or conversion technique, this guide takes priority.

## Core voice

The FishWithLocals voice is:

- **Warm.** We welcome people without performing friendliness.
- **Honest.** We state what is known, admit limits and never promise what the sea cannot provide.
- **Specific.** We name the person, place, action, object or moment rather than reaching for a broad claim.
- **Observant.** We notice hands, habits, weather, pauses, food, working rhythms and the details that reveal character.
- **Calm.** We do not rush the reader or create excitement for its own sake.
- **Quietly confident.** We explain what we know without boasting or comparing ourselves with others.
- **Respectful of the sea.** The sea is a living presence and a working environment, not scenery to be consumed.
- **Never exaggerated.** We do not enlarge personalities, catches, traditions or environmental impact.
- **Never sales-driven.** We help a visitor understand and choose; we do not push them towards a transaction.

Warm does not mean sentimental. Calm does not mean vague. Specific writing can be simple and still carry feeling.

## Writing principles

### Show, do not claim

Replace adjectives with evidence. Do not call a captain patient when a true moment can show how they slowed down to help a child tie the same knot three times. Do not call someone responsible when a verified habit can show that they bring floating plastic back to shore.

### Use concrete details

Prefer names, actions, objects and places. “Coffee from a small metal pot before sunrise” gives the reader something to understand. “Exceptional local hospitality” does not.

Details must earn their place. Include them because they reveal the person, place or experience, not merely to make prose decorative.

### Write from real conversations

Captain profiles and quotations begin with what a captain actually said. Stories begin with a witnessed event, a reliable account or a real observation. Editing may improve order and clarity, but it must not change meaning.

If a sentence could apply to any captain in any coastal town, return to the conversation and ask for something more precise.

### Avoid generic tourism language

Do not describe every harbour as picturesque, every coastline as breathtaking or every day as unforgettable. Such words flatten places instead of revealing them. Explain what happens there, who works there, what changes with the season and what a visitor might notice.

### Do not enlarge people into characters

Never make a captain sound more heroic, rustic, humorous, ecological, wise or poetic than they are. A profile is not folklore. Preserve contradictions and ordinary qualities when they are true. A quiet answer may be more revealing than a polished one.

### Let small details carry meaning

A repaired glove, a child’s first knot, fruit shared after a slow morning or a captain pausing to give another boat room can express more than a paragraph of claims. Trust the reader to understand the meaning without explaining it twice.

### Never manufacture personality

Do not invent quotations, habits, jokes, preferences or emotional reactions. Do not assign a tone to a captain because it suits the page. If the conversation did not reveal enough, speak with the captain again.

### Respect silence and space

Not every paragraph needs a flourish, and not every photograph needs a long caption. Use short paragraphs. Allow a plain sentence to stand. Remove text that repeats an idea already carried by an image or detail.

## Words and phrases we avoid

### Luxury and inflated experience language

Avoid:

- luxury
- premium experience
- unique experience
- unforgettable experience
- authentic experience

These terms make different people and places sound interchangeable. They promise a feeling instead of describing what is actually offered. “Authentic” is especially weak when the page should demonstrate authenticity through real names, habits and facts.

### Generic tourism labels

Avoid:

- hidden gem
- must-see
- breathtaking
- bucket-list
- paradise

These phrases treat a place as something to collect. They say little about its people, working life or relationship with the sea.

### Rankings and superiority claims

Avoid:

- best
- number one
- top-rated
- leading
- most popular

FishWithLocals does not place captains or destinations in a contest. Use verified facts and allow visitors to decide what suits them.

### Pressure and booking language

Avoid:

- book now
- reserve now
- limited spots
- only two places left
- hurry
- best deal
- do not miss out
- catch guaranteed

The platform begins a direct conversation; it does not sell inventory. Conditions and catches cannot be guaranteed. Urgency weakens trust.

### Corporate and technology language

Avoid:

- synergy
- disruption
- cutting-edge
- seamless
- AI-powered
- ecosystem, unless it is scientifically and contextually accurate

These words draw attention to systems rather than people. Use ordinary language that explains what happens.

### Vague collective language

Avoid **community** unless it refers to a real local community in a concrete way. Name the people where possible: marina workers, fishers in Bar, families who prepare a particular food, or captains who share the same harbour. A broad claim about “supporting the community” requires evidence.

Use **ecosystem** only when describing an ecological system accurately. Do not use it as a fashionable substitute for network, group or platform.

## Preferred alternatives

### Experience claims

**Weak:** “Enjoy an unforgettable premium fishing experience.”

**Better:** “Leave the harbour with someone who knows these waters by name.”

The second version gives the reader a person, an action and a relationship with place.

### Urgency

**Weak:** “Book now before places sell out.”

**Better:** “Meet the captain and start a conversation.”

The second version describes the real next step without pressure.

### Environmental claims

**Weak:** “Eco-friendly fishing trip.”

**Better:** “Feta brings a rubbish bag on every trip and brings floating plastic back to shore.”

The second version describes a specific action. Publish it only after verifying that it is true.

### Captain personality

**Weak:** “A passionate local captain who provides outstanding service.”

**Better:** “When a beginner misses a fish, Marko resets the line and explains what changed in the current.”

### Destination writing

**Weak:** “Discover Budva, a hidden gem with breathtaking views.”

**Better:** “In Budva, fishing boats leave beside a waterfront that changes character before the town wakes.”

### Slow fishing

**Weak:** “An amazing catch is guaranteed.”

**Better:** “Some mornings are quiet. When they are, the captain changes method, explains the water and lets the day become about more than a fish.”

### Food

**Weak:** “Enjoy a premium local dining experience on board.”

**Better:** “The captain brings bread, tomatoes and cheese, and makes coffee when the boat settles.”

### Verification

**Weak:** “Only the highest-quality providers join our platform.”

**Better:** “We meet every captain, inspect the boat, check the relevant documents and review the profile together before publication.”

## Captain profiles

A captain profile is a faithful introduction to a person. It is not a listing with personality added afterwards.

Build the profile from the Captain’s Conversation, observed details and verified facts. Include the captain’s relationship with the sea, local knowledge, fishing methods, habits, humour, teaching style, food, hospitality, values and response to difficult days. Practical information—languages, group guidance, boat, safety, price, inclusions and departure point—must be clear but should not overwhelm the person.

Use quotations sparingly and accurately. A captain should recognise their own meaning and manner in the finished profile. Review sensitive details and the final text with them.

Do not turn captains into stock characters: the weathered old fisherman, the cheerful host, the fearless hunter or the perfect environmental guardian. Do not tidy away every complexity. A person can love fishing, keep fish for food and release others thoughtfully. Write the truth without forcing it into a simpler role.

If personality is missing, do not invent it. Return to the conversation.

## Destination pages

Write Bar, Budva and future destinations as lived-in places rather than products. Begin with what is particular: working rhythms around the marina, weather, food, habits, local people, seasonal changes and the relationship between the town and the sea.

A destination page should help a visitor understand where they are going, not merely persuade them to go. Include useful information about departure points, access, seasons and what the day may feel like. Place practical guidance beside stories and observations.

Avoid generic descriptions that could be transferred between towns. Do not call a place untouched when people live and work there. Do not describe local life as a spectacle for visitors. Never claim that one destination is more authentic than another.

Use local names correctly. Verify history, customs and statements about tradition. When one person’s memory or habit is presented, identify it as theirs rather than speaking for an entire town.

## Stories

Stories are not blog posts produced to fill a publishing calendar, and they are not SEO padding. A story should exist because someone noticed something worth remembering.

Stories should be specific, quiet and complete enough to reward attention. Not every story needs a fish or a dramatic ending. A story may follow:

- a child releasing a fish
- a tuna visible for one second
- a squid hunting an arbun
- rubbish brought back to shore
- coffee in the marina
- a captain washing the boat
- weather changing the day
- silence at sea

Begin close to the moment. Use only the background needed to understand it. Let uncertainty remain when it is honest. Do not add a moral to every ending or force a small event to carry a grand message.

A useful story leaves the reader knowing one person, place or habit more clearly than before.

## Responsible fishing and respect for the sea

Show responsibility through verified behaviour, not badges, slogans or broad environmental claims.

Write about what someone does: releases a fish when appropriate, respects a season, keeps only what will be used, gives another boat’s lines room, carries rubbish ashore, collects floating plastic safely or retrieves abandoned line. Explain context when a decision depends on species, condition, law or local practice.

Avoid “sustainable”, “green” and “eco-friendly” unless a precise, supportable meaning is provided. Never imply that one small action solves a larger environmental problem. Ordinary responsible conduct can be described without turning a captain into a hero.

The sea should never appear only as a blue background. It changes plans, teaches judgement, provides food and work, and sets limits. In FishWithLocals writing, the sea is one of the main characters.

## Calls to action

Acceptable public calls to action are:

- **Meet the Captains**
- **Talk to the Captain**
- **Start the Conversation**
- **Explore Bar**
- **Explore Budva**
- **Read the Story**
- **Learn How We Verify Captains**

These actions invite attention or conversation and accurately describe what happens next.

Avoid:

- **Book Now**
- **Reserve Now**
- **Hurry**
- **Limited Availability**
- **Best Deal**

Use calls to action sparingly. One clear action is better than several competing buttons. Do not repeat an action merely to increase pressure.

## Mobile reading and responsive content

FishWithLocals is designed for desktop and mobile. Desktop may be the reference layout during design, but mobile must never be an afterthought. Every page must remain comfortable to read on a phone.

Follow these editorial rules:

- Keep paragraphs short. One to four sentences is usually enough on a small screen.
- Use clear, descriptive section headings so a reader can find their place.
- Break up long blocks of text before they become tiring.
- Place the most important idea early. Do not make the reader cross an introduction to discover the point.
- Keep buttons clear but calm. Labels should say what happens next.
- Do not overload the first screen with text, controls, badges or competing actions.
- Ensure every image still carries meaning when cropped or reduced.
- Write captions that add a fact, name, place or observation. Do not use captions as decoration.
- Avoid autoplay video, heavy animation and unnecessary media.
- Assume some visitors are browsing from marinas, beaches, hotels or slower mobile connections.
- Treat download size, image weight and page speed as editorial decisions, not only technical ones.

Speed is part of hospitality. A story that takes too long to load has not welcomed the reader well.

> Every page should answer: how does this feel on a phone held in one hand?

During editorial review, read the page on an actual phone. Check paragraph length, heading rhythm, image crops, captions and the distance between the reader’s question and its answer. A page is not finished because it fits; it is finished when it remains calm and understandable.

## Translation and multilingual writing

Montenegrin and English versions should carry the same meaning, not mirror one another word for word. Natural tone matters more than preserving sentence structure.

Translate the person’s intent, degree of warmth, humour and directness. Do not make the English version more promotional or the Montenegrin version more formal. Read each version as writing in its own language.

Local expressions may remain when they reveal character or place. Explain them briefly when a visitor would otherwise miss the meaning. Preserve names of foods, tools, winds and customs where the local word carries useful knowledge, but do not leave the reader guessing merely for atmosphere.

Quotations require particular care. If a polished translation loses the captain’s character, choose a simpler sentence closer to how they speak.

## Final editorial test

Before publishing any text, ask:

1. Is this true?
2. Is it specific?
3. Did it come from a real person, place or observation?
4. Does it respect the captain?
5. Does it respect the sea?
6. Would we still publish it if there were no marketing benefit?
7. Does it feel calm and readable on a phone?
8. Does it help someone choose with trust rather than pressure?

If any answer is uncertain, pause. Verify the fact, return to the source conversation, simplify the wording or remove the claim. Publishing less is better than publishing something we cannot stand behind.
