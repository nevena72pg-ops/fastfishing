# Captain’s Conversation

**Version 1.1**

## How to use this conversation

There are no correct answers. Take your time. Stories are more valuable than short replies. If one memory leads to another, follow it. Some of the most important answers are the ones nobody expected. The conversation should feel like two people talking over coffee in a marina, not completing a questionnaire.

This is not an employment interview, a test or a form to complete quickly. It is a way to spend time with a future FishWithLocals captain before writing their profile.

Let the conversation wander when something interesting appears. Ask for examples. Leave room for pauses, detours and stories that were not expected. The order is only a guide, and not every question needs to be asked in one sitting.

Listen for the person behind the answers: their humour, patience, values, habits, hospitality, teaching style and relationship with the sea. Record their meaning accurately. A profile should come from what the captain has genuinely shared, never from what we imagine they might have meant.

## Early memories

1. What is your earliest clear memory of being near the sea?
2. Tell me about the person who first made the sea feel familiar to you.
3. Which sounds, smells or small details take you back to those early years?
4. When did you first realise that the sea would remain an important part of your life?

## The sea

5. How has your understanding of the sea changed as you have grown older?
6. What can you notice in the water or sky now that you could not see when you began?
7. Tell me about a time when the sea changed your plans and taught you something.
8. Where do you feel most at home on the water, and what makes that place different?
9. Tell me about something you have seen at sea that still amazes you today.

## Fishing

10. How would you describe the way you like to fish to someone who has never held a rod?
11. Which fishing method gives you the greatest satisfaction, and what do you enjoy about it?
12. Tell me about a fish that taught you patience, respect or humility.
13. How do seasons change the way you think about fishing here?
14. What separates a thoughtful angler from someone who simply wants to catch as much as possible?

## The rhythm of a day

15. Walk me through the quiet part of your morning before the guests arrive.
16. What do you check, prepare or notice before deciding that the boat is ready?
17. How does the mood of a day usually change between leaving the marina and returning?
18. Which ordinary task on the boat do you enjoy more than most people would expect?

## Hospitality

19. What helps a stranger begin to feel comfortable on your boat?
20. How do you learn what kind of day a particular guest is hoping for?
21. Tell me about a guest who arrived nervous or uncertain and gradually relaxed.
22. What does good hospitality at sea mean to you when there is no need to impress anyone?

## Food

23. What food or drink naturally belongs to a day on your boat?
24. Tell me about something you enjoy preparing or sharing with guests.
25. Which local dish carries a memory or story for you?
26. How does food change the feeling between people after several hours together at sea?

## Teaching

27. How do you recognise when a guest wants to learn rather than simply watch?
28. What do you most enjoy explaining: knots, rigs, weather, fish behaviour, navigation, or something else?
29. Tell me about a moment when someone understood something because of the way you showed them.
30. What has the sea taught you recently that you are still trying to understand?

## Children

31. How do you help a child feel safe and included during their first time on a fishing boat?
32. What changes in your way of teaching when a child is holding the rod?
33. Tell me about a child whose curiosity stayed with you after the day ended.
34. What would you like a child to understand about the sea before stepping back onto land?

## Difficult days

35. What do you do when the fish seem to have disappeared completely?
36. Tell me about a difficult day that became worthwhile for a reason other than the catch.
37. How do you speak with guests when weather, timing or conditions force the plan to change?
38. What has a mistake or poor decision at sea taught you about judgement?

## Humour and personality

39. What usually makes you laugh during a long day on the water?
40. Tell me about a moment on board that became funnier each time you retold it.
41. What kind of music, conversation or silence feels natural on your boat?
42. Do you enjoy silence at sea, or do you prefer conversation? How do you know which one your guests need?
43. Which of your habits would your friends recognise immediately if they joined you at sea?

## Respect

44. What does taking only what is needed look like in your own fishing?
45. How do you decide when a fish should be released?
46. Tell me about an everyday action that shows care for the sea without needing attention or praise.
47. What do you usually do when you find rubbish floating at sea or caught in your fishing gear?
48. What courtesies between captains and other boats matter most in these waters?
49. Which behaviour at sea causes you to lose respect for someone?

## Local traditions

50. Which fishing practice, expression or custom feels closely tied to this place?
51. Tell me about a piece of local knowledge that was passed to you rather than written down.
52. Which tradition has changed during your lifetime, and how do you feel about that change?
53. What should a visitor understand about local life before they think they understand local fishing?

## Personal philosophy

54. How do you know when a day at sea has been a good one?
55. What has fishing taught you about patience beyond the boat?
56. Which principle guides you when nobody is watching?
57. What are you still curious to learn from the sea?
58. Has there ever been a moment at sea that you still think about years later? Tell me about it.

## Looking back

59. When you look back over your years on the water, which moment best explains why you still return?
60. What would you like the next generation of local captains to protect or carry forward?
61. If someone who had never met you spent one day on your boat, what would you hope they remembered years later?
62. If one child left your boat loving the sea more than when they arrived, would you consider the day a success? Why?

## Interviewer’s notes

After the conversation finishes, write down three things that surprised you.

Do not summarise the interview.

Record only the moments that made you think:

> “I did not expect that answer.”

These moments often become the heart of the captain’s profile.
