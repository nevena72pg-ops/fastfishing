# Captain Handbook

**Version 1.1**

## Purpose

This handbook defines the minimum standard for every captain represented by FishWithLocals. It is for the people who find captains, meet them, verify their information, write their profiles and maintain those relationships.

Our role is not to collect as many captains as possible. We must know each captain well enough to represent them honestly. A good boat, a large following or an impressive catch is not a reason to publish someone. When something is uncertain, record it as uncertain. When it cannot be verified, do not publish it as fact.

## Selection criteria

A FishWithLocals captain must:

- Have genuine local knowledge of the waters in which they operate.
- Have the experience and permissions required for the activity offered.
- Put safety and sound judgement ahead of pleasing a guest.
- Explain prices, inclusions, limitations and conditions honestly.
- Treat guests, other captains and the sea with respect.
- Be comfortable being presented as a person, not simply a boat operator.
- Be willing to discuss slow days and the limits of an experience.
- Keep their public information accurate.

We look for consistency between what a captain says, what others know of them, how they maintain their boat and how they behave at sea. Charisma in one meeting is not enough.

## Verification process

Verification is an ongoing responsibility, not a badge awarded once. Before publication, a FishWithLocals team member should:

1. Discuss the captain’s background, work, values and proposed experiences.
2. Meet the captain in person whenever reasonably possible.
3. Confirm identity and private contact information.
4. Inspect the vessel and record its basic details.
5. Review relevant credentials, vessel documents and permissions.
6. Discuss safety equipment, emergencies, passenger limits and weather decisions.
7. Check local reputation through appropriate people who know the captain’s work.
8. Discuss fishing methods, treatment of catch, seasons and care for the sea.
9. Confirm prices, duration, group limits, inclusions, exclusions and departure arrangements.
10. Complete the personality interview and review the finished profile with the captain.

Record who performed each check, when, and when it should be repeated. Keep evidence private. Reverify when documents expire, a vessel or experience changes, credible concerns arise, or our knowledge is no longer current.

## Values

We look for conduct rather than polished answers. Captains may be quiet or talkative, traditional or modern. What matters is honesty, patience, judgement, unforced hospitality and respect for the sea.

A captain must be able to say no to unsafe weather, unsuitable requests, excessive catch or disrespectful behaviour. FishWithLocals supports that judgement rather than pressuring a captain to protect an inquiry.

## Curiosity and teaching

FishWithLocals captains enjoy sharing what they know. Guests should feel comfortable asking about fishing methods, knots and rigs, weather, currents, local traditions, fish behaviour, navigation and life at sea.

Teaching is not lecturing. It is noticing curiosity, making room for questions and encouraging someone to look more closely. Children, especially, should leave the boat knowing something they did not know before boarding. The experience is about learning the sea, not only catching fish.

Captains are not expected to know everything. We value those who remain curious, continue learning from the sea and enjoy sharing that learning honestly with guests.

## Photography standards

Photography should help a guest recognise the real captain, vessel and setting. Aim for a natural portrait, the captain at work, the vessel as it normally appears, realistic guest space, and details that reveal character or place.

Look for ordinary moments that tell an honest story: hands at work, knots, lines, cooking, preparing gear and the small details of daily life at sea.

Avoid stock photographs, artificial scenes and heavy editing. Never make a vessel look larger or imply facilities that are absent. Catch images must avoid unnecessary blood, waste, prolonged handling or piles of fish staged to suggest abundance. Never stage irresponsible behaviour for a stronger image.

Obtain permission from identifiable adults. Images of children require permission from a parent or guardian. Record the photographer and usage permission.

## Communication style

Captains should communicate directly, clearly and without sales pressure. Before a day is agreed, the guest should understand who the captain is, the likely departure details, price, inclusions, exclusions, duration, group limit, type of experience, what to bring, and how conditions may change the plan. Fish and specific species are never guaranteed.

Messages should receive a reasonable response, even if it is only a note that the captain is at sea. Changes should be communicated early. Captain and guest must speak directly before the experience.

## Guest expectations

The public description must match the real day. Do not call an experience suitable for families, beginners, children or guests with limited mobility unless the captain and vessel can genuinely support them.

Guests must understand that sea conditions and fishing activity cannot be controlled. The captain may change the route, method, timing or duration. Safety comes first. A worthwhile day should still contain attention, knowledge and care when fishing is slow.

## Respect for the sea

Responsible fishing appears in routine decisions: respecting seasons, protected species, legal sizes and catch limits; releasing fish when appropriate; taking no more than can be used; avoiding sensitive areas; carrying rubbish ashore; collecting floating plastic when safe; recovering abandoned line or nets when practical; and preventing avoidable fuel, oil, hook and line pollution.

We describe these actions plainly. Ordinary responsible behaviour should not be turned into heroism.

## Respect for other captains

FishWithLocals does not rank captains. Represented captains respect other boats, working space, fishing lines and established local practice. They do not attack another captain to win a guest. Genuine concerns are raised privately and factually.

We never publish claims such as “the best”, “number one” or “better than everyone else”.

## Children on board

“Children welcome” must be verified. Confirm practical age guidance, correctly sized flotation equipment, safe seating and movement, shade and weather limitations, the captain’s experience with children, adult supervision, and safe management of hooks, knives and lines.

The captain must know children’s ages in advance and may decline or change a plan when the vessel, weather, duration or child’s needs make it unsuitable. A child’s first fishing experience should never become a stressful memory. Patience, encouragement and curiosity matter more than catching fish.

## Catch and release

Catch and release is not automatically right in every circumstance, and keeping a fish is not automatically wrong. Decisions depend on species, season, condition, method, local rules and whether the catch will be used.

Captains should minimise handling time, use suitable equipment and avoid keeping fish out of water merely for photographs. Fish that will not be used should not be taken for display. Public claims must match practice on board.

## Local traditions

Local methods, food and stories belong when they are real and freely shared. Do not invent folklore, exaggerate age or importance, or present one person’s habit as the custom of an entire place. Do not expose private fishing grounds, sensitive knowledge or stories without permission.

Tradition never excuses unsafe, unlawful or damaging practice.

## Safety

Verify vessel identity, capacity and condition; relevant registrations and permissions; suitable flotation equipment; communication, emergency, first-aid and fire equipment; weather and cancellation judgement; guest safety briefings; and important vessel limitations.

We do not certify that an activity is risk-free. We verify specific facts and record when they were checked. A serious incident, credible safety concern or material equipment change pauses the profile until reviewed.

## Food

Describe food as it really appears. Meals are moments of hospitality rather than restaurant service. Simple local food, honestly prepared, is more valuable than exaggerated promises. Verify what is normally provided, what depends on the catch, and where preparation happens. Never promise a meal based on catching fish that day.

Guests must be asked about allergies and dietary needs. If a need cannot be accommodated safely, say so clearly.

## Storytelling

Profiles should sound like the captain, not a copywriter’s idea of one. Use details gathered in conversation: first memories of the sea, teachers, difficult days, food, humour, music, patience and what the captain wants guests to remember. Confirm quotations and sensitive stories.

Never make someone seem more rustic, heroic, eccentric or environmentally virtuous than they are. Editing and translation may improve clarity but must not manufacture personality.

## Things we never publish

- Identity, licence or document numbers.
- Home addresses or unnecessary family information.
- Private contact details without permission.
- Verification documents.
- Exact sensitive fishing grounds.
- Rumours or unverified accusations.
- Catch, weather or safety guarantees.
- Rankings or superiority claims.
- Invented quotations, stories or personality details.
- Images or information without permission.
- Details exposing protected species, sensitive habitats or unsafe practice.

## Things we always verify

- Captain identity, contact details and the person leading the experience.
- Relevant credentials and permissions.
- Vessel identity, capacity and current use.
- Core safety equipment and procedures.
- Destinations and departure points.
- Group limits and suitability claims.
- Prices, currency, pricing unit, inclusions and exclusions.
- Duration and weather arrangements.
- Languages spoken.
- Claims concerning children, accessibility and food.
- Public responsible-fishing claims.
- Photography permissions.
- The captain’s approval of the final profile.

## Ongoing responsibility

Publication is not the end of verification. Keep contact, review important information and respond seriously to credible feedback. Correct inaccuracies promptly. Pause a profile when a concern affects safety, trust or identity. Remove a captain when our standards are no longer met or we can no longer stand behind the profile.

The final question is simple: do we know this captain well enough to introduce them to someone we care about? If the answer is uncertain, the profile is not ready.
