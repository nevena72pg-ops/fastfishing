# Homepage Concept

**Version 1.0**

## Overall feeling

The homepage should feel calm enough for a visitor to pay attention. It should not behave like a catalogue competing for quick clicks. The pace is unhurried, the page has room to breathe, and each section gives one idea enough space before introducing the next.

The emotional centre is trust. A visitor should sense that the people shown here are known personally, that the writing has come from real conversations, and that the sea is not being offered as something to consume. Fishing is present, but so are patience, local knowledge, food, weather, humour, teaching and the ordinary rhythm of a day on the water.

The page should feel human rather than luxurious, and considered rather than polished for its own sake. It should avoid the visual habits of both tourism marketplaces and technology companies: no crowded search controls, oversized promises, glossy yachts, numerical proof or constant demands to act. The difference should be understood through restraint, not announced through self-congratulation.

## Hero section

The first impression should be a real encounter with a captain and the sea. The hero should not begin with a booking form, destination search or row of filters. It should begin with a person in a recognisable, unforced moment: preparing gear in early light, looking towards changing water, making coffee, handling a line or talking with someone on board.

The headline should express the founding philosophy in plain language. Its role is to establish that visitors choose a captain, not simply a boat or trip. It should be short and grounded, without claims about being unique, unforgettable, exclusive or the best.

Supporting text should explain only what is needed at this point: FishWithLocals introduces travellers to personally verified local captains in Bar and Budva, and the conversation happens directly. It should also leave room for the larger truth that a day at sea is about more than catching fish.

The primary action should invite the visitor to meet the captains. A quieter secondary path may lead to Bar and Budva. Neither action should use booking language. “Talk to the Captain”, “Meet the Captains”, “Explore Bar” and “Explore Budva” are appropriate directions because they describe what actually happens.

The photography must be original. Use real captains, their real boats and natural light. Prefer an image with texture and context over a perfect horizon. Avoid stock imagery, staged celebration, drone spectacle, trophy catches and photographs that make a modest boat look like a luxury charter. The image should hold up even if no fish appears in it.

## What visitors discover next

Immediately after the hero, the visitor should understand how trust is created. A short, spacious passage should explain that captains are met personally, important facts are checked, profiles are written from conversation, and guests speak directly with the captain. This is not a long “how it works” diagram. Three concise ideas are enough: known personally, represented honestly, contacted directly.

The captain should come before destinations, experience categories or stories. This order matters. The premise of FishWithLocals is not that Bar and Budva contain products to browse; it is that real people can help a visitor understand and experience these waters. Showing captains early turns the philosophy into evidence.

Stories should follow the first captain introduction. They deepen the impression created by the profiles and demonstrate that the platform notices more than practical details. Destinations come afterwards, giving geographical and cultural context to people the visitor has already begun to recognise.

The natural page flow is therefore:

1. A quiet human introduction in the hero.
2. A brief explanation of personal verification and direct conversation.
3. A small, carefully presented group of captains.
4. Stories that reveal character, knowledge and daily life.
5. Bar and Budva as lived-in destinations.
6. Respect for the sea, shown through conduct.
7. A final, low-pressure invitation to continue.

## Captain section

Captains should be introduced as people with distinct temperaments, not as interchangeable service providers. The section should show only a small number at once. A limited selection suggests care and allows each person enough room to be understood.

Each introduction should lead with a natural portrait, the captain’s name and a brief human detail drawn from the Captain’s Conversation. This might concern patience with beginners, a morning ritual, a way of teaching, food shared on board, dry humour, or what the captain does when the fish are not biting. The detail must be true and specific; it should never be manufactured to make the card more appealing.

Destination, languages and the kind of day offered can appear as supporting information. Verification should be visible but quiet, with a clear route to understanding what it means. Boat information belongs further down the hierarchy. Ratings, rankings, review counts, popularity labels and price-led comparison do not belong here.

The layout should encourage reading one captain at a time rather than scanning for the largest boat or lowest price. Portraits should not be cropped into identical commercial headshots. Differences in age, expression, working style and surroundings are part of the truth.

The action from each captain introduction should be an invitation to meet or learn about that person. Starting a conversation belongs on the full profile, after the visitor has had enough context to make a thoughtful choice.

## Stories

Stories deserve a prominent place because facts establish clarity while stories establish recognition. A visitor may forget a vessel length but remember the captain who learnt a knot from their grandfather, brings fruit from a neighbour’s garden, or still thinks about a particular night at sea.

The homepage should present a small editorial selection rather than a busy content feed. Two or three stories are enough. They may concern a captain, local person, fishing practice, meal, place, changing season, conservation habit or piece of knowledge passed down by hand. Their purpose is not to keep visitors scrolling. It is to reveal how FishWithLocals pays attention.

Story photography should favour ordinary moments: hands repairing gear, line being prepared, coffee beside a coil of rope, cooking, weather moving across the coast, or two people listening to one another. Headlines should be plain and specific, never mysterious for the sake of a click.

These stories build trust before a traveller makes any practical arrangement. They show that the captain is not a name attached to availability, and that the destination is not a backdrop. The visitor begins to understand the people and place before asking for anything from them.

## Destinations

Bar and Budva should be presented as places with history, working rhythms, people, food, traditions and different relationships with the sea. They are not product categories and should not be reduced to a photograph, a count of captains and a button.

Each destination introduction should contain a short editorial observation that could only belong to that place. Photography should include the marina, shoreline, working boats and daily life, not only postcard views. Practical information matters, but it belongs on the destination page alongside local stories, marina guidance, seasonality and responsible-fishing context.

The homepage should give Bar and Budva equal dignity without forcing them into identical descriptions. Their differences should emerge naturally. No destination should be described as superior, more exclusive or more authentic than the other.

## Responsible relationship with the sea

Respect for the sea should be visible throughout the homepage rather than isolated inside an “eco” panel. A separate green section with badges and broad claims would turn conduct into branding.

Show real actions instead. A caption might describe a fish being released appropriately, rubbish brought back to shore, abandoned line recovered safely, a seasonal limit respected, or another boat’s working space left undisturbed. A captain’s own words can explain why they take only what will be used. A story may show a child learning to handle a fish carefully or to notice a change in the water.

The tone should remain factual and modest. These actions are part of good seamanship and responsible fishing, not evidence that the platform has solved an environmental problem. Where a practice is shown, it must have been observed or verified.

## Calls to action

The homepage needs only a few invitations:

- Meet the captains.
- Explore Bar or Budva.
- Read a story.
- Learn how captains are verified.
- Start a conversation with a chosen captain.

Actions should appear when the visitor has enough context for them. The hero may invite exploration; captain profiles may invite a closer look; the final section may invite a conversation. Repeating the same button after every paragraph would create pressure and weaken the calm of the page.

There should be no countdown, urgency, scarcity message or suggestion that another visitor is about to take something away. No action should imply an instant reservation. The visitor is free to read, leave and return later.

## Visual language

### Typography

Use a readable serif with warmth and quiet character for major headings and selected editorial passages. Pair it with a restrained sans serif for navigation, labels and practical information. Typography should feel established without imitating an old maritime museum. Avoid fashionable display type, exaggerated contrast and all-capital shouting.

### Colour palette

The palette should come from the actual coast: deep water blue, weathered stone, off-white canvas, muted rope and restrained greens or rust tones found in working harbours. Colours should feel sun-worn rather than digitally bright. Maintain strong text contrast and never rely on colour alone to communicate meaning.

### Spacing

Generous spacing is essential. Sections should be separated by rhythm, not heavy boxes. Text columns should remain comfortable to read, and mobile layouts should preserve pauses rather than compress everything into a stream of cards.

### Photography

Photography carries most of the emotional weight. Use documentary observation, natural light and ordinary moments. Show faces, hands, tools, weather, food and working details. Keep imperfections that make the scene believable. Captions should add knowledge rather than repeat what is visible.

### Illustrations and icons

Illustration should be rare and purposeful. Simple drawings based on knots, coastlines, species or working objects may support explanation when they are accurate and locally grounded. Avoid decorative anchors, steering wheels, waves and other generic nautical symbols.

Icons should be limited to practical meaning, such as language, group guidance or duration. They must remain subordinate to words and should not turn captain profiles into specification sheets.

### Movement

Movement should be minimal and slow. Gentle transitions may help orientation, but nothing should compete with reading. Respect reduced-motion preferences. Avoid parallax spectacle, animated counters, looping backgrounds and autoplay media. Stillness is part of the design language.

## What we deliberately avoid

- Countdown timers and “only two places left” messages, because the platform does not manufacture urgency.
- Flashing discounts, promotional ribbons and crossed-out prices, because transparent pricing is not a sales performance.
- Review competitions, star ratings and popularity rankings, because captains are not being placed in a contest.
- Algorithm-driven recommendations, because personal fit should not be replaced by opaque ranking.
- Intrusive pop-ups and immediate newsletter demands, because attention should be earned rather than captured.
- Autoplay video or sound, because visitors should control the pace and atmosphere.
- Clickbait headlines, because stories deserve accurate names.
- Large search boxes and dense filters in the hero, because the first question is who the captain is, not how quickly inventory can be narrowed.
- Rotating carousels, because they hide people and encourage hurried scanning.
- Luxury charter imagery, polished lifestyle staging and trophy-catch galleries, because they distort the ordinary character of the experience.
- Generic environmental badges, because respect must be shown through verified behaviour.
- Social-proof counters, recent-activity messages and conversion prompts, because trust cannot be hurried into existence.

These patterns do not belong on FishWithLocals because each one pushes the visitor towards speed, comparison or anxiety. The platform asks for the opposite: attention, judgement and enough time to choose a person well.

## Final thought

A visitor should be able to close the homepage without starting a conversation and still feel that the visit was worthwhile. They should leave with the sense that Bar and Budva contain real people who know their waters, and that those people have been represented with care. The page has succeeded when it creates trust and curiosity without demanding a decision—when the visitor remembers a face, a habit or a story rather than a tourism product.
